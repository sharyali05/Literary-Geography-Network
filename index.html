01 — Motivation

Motivation

"Geography is not the inert container of historical events — it is one of the forces that shapes them." — Franco Moretti, Atlas of the European Novel (1998)

The 19th century sits at a profound tension point in European literary history. On one hand, print culture and publishing were becoming increasingly centralized in a handful of major cities — Paris, London, Leipzig. On the other hand, nationalist literary movements were actively constructing distinct regional and national voices, from Russian Realism to Scandinavian Naturalism to the Italian Risorgimento.

This project builds a network to arbitrate between two competing hypotheses about how geography related to literary production:

The Hub Hypothesis

A small number of dominant cities acted as gravitational centers. Literary influence flowed outward from Paris, London, and Berlin. Authors had to pass through these hubs to gain legitimacy.

The Cluster Hypothesis

Distinct regional literary scenes developed in relative isolation. Geography reinforced movement boundaries rather than dissolving them, producing tight local clusters with weak inter-regional ties.

The theoretical grounding comes from Moretti's argument that geography is constitutive of literature, and Casanova's claim in The World Republic of Letters (1999) that Paris functioned as the dominant "meridian" of literary prestige — a claim this network can empirically test.

02 — Dataset

Dataset

The dataset is constructed by querying the Wikidata SPARQL endpoint for European authors born between 1760 and 1880, collecting birth cities, residence cities, literary movement affiliations, and geographic coordinates. This yields a bipartite graph connecting authors to the cities they inhabited.

Coverage spans 19 European countries including France, Britain, Germany, Russia, Italy, Spain, Austria, and Scandinavia. A curated fallback dataset of ~50 canonical authors is included for cases where the Wikidata API is unavailable.

03 — Approach

Approach & Network Metrics

The analysis proceeds in three stages: constructing the bipartite network, projecting it into two unipartite networks, and computing metrics and communities on each.

MetricApplied toWhat it tells us
Degree Centrality City network Which cities attracted the most authors? Identifies gravitational hubs.
Betweenness Centrality City + Author networks Which nodes act as bridges between otherwise disconnected literary regions? Often more surprising than degree.
Closeness Centrality City network Which cities could most efficiently "reach" the rest of the literary world?
Louvain Community Detection Both networks Do communities map onto geographic regions, national traditions, or literary movements?
Modularity Score Both networks How strongly does the network partition into distinct communities? Higher = stronger geographic clustering.
Attribute Assortativity Author network Do same-movement authors share cities? Tests whether geography reinforced or cut across movement identity.
Epoch Subgraphs City network Comparing 1760–1829 vs. 1830–1889 reveals whether the geographic center of literary production shifted over the century.

The bipartite design is a deliberate methodological choice: rather than claiming authors "interacted" (which would require direct evidence), we connect them through shared cities — a verifiable, well-documented relationship. Edge weights distinguish birth cities (1.5) from residence cities (1.0), reflecting the stronger claim of long-term habitation.

05 — Discussion

Discussion

The network does not merely illustrate what historians already know — it surfaces the structural logic beneath the literary map of 19th-century Europe.

The degree centrality results reveal which cities truly dominated as attractors of literary talent. The more analytically interesting finding comes from betweenness centrality: certain mid-sized cities score disproportionately high because they connect otherwise separate literary regions — cities like Geneva or Brussels, sitting at the intersection of French and German literary worlds, may outperform larger capitals as structural bridges.

The Louvain community structure tests the core hypothesis: if communities map onto geographic regions rather than literary movements, geography was the stronger organizing principle. If communities cut across geography and align with movements (Romanticism spanning France, Germany, and Britain as one cluster), then shared aesthetics mattered more than place.

The movement assortativity coefficient directly measures whether same-movement authors co-located. A positive value supports Moretti's geographic determinism; a negative value would suggest that literary movements were inherently cosmopolitan — that Realism, for instance, was something authors encountered in Paris regardless of where they came from.

06 — Limitations

Limitations

  • Coverage bias: Wikidata's documentation is uneven; French and British authors are over-represented, while Eastern European and Iberian authors are systematically undercounted, which may artificially inflate the centrality of Paris and London.
  • Temporal conflation: The bipartite projection merges authors across the entire century. An author resident in Paris in 1810 and one resident there in 1890 appear structurally equivalent, despite no possibility of overlap.
  • Definitional inconsistency: "City of residence" in Wikidata conflates brief visits, years-long stays, and permanent settlements without distinction.
  • Projection information loss: Moving from the bipartite network to unipartite projections discards directionality, timing, and individual connection strength.
  • Proximity ≠ influence: Geographic co-presence is not evidence of intellectual relationship, correspondence, or mutual influence. The network captures structural proximity, not documented interaction.
07 — Further Study

Further Study

  • Correspondence networks: Enrich with digitized author letters from the EMLO database to build a direct social network on top of the geographic one — transforming proximity evidence into relationship evidence.
  • Dynamic network analysis: Replace two coarse epoch slices with decade-level or year-level snapshots to track the emergence and decline of individual cities as literary hubs in near-real-time.
  • Global expansion: Extend the dataset beyond Europe to test whether the hub structure (and Paris's dominance in particular) was a specifically European phenomenon or part of a global literary system.
  • Stylometric layer: Use NLP on Project Gutenberg texts to test whether geographic proximity correlates with measurable stylistic similarity — connecting the network analysis to the texts themselves.
  • Publisher networks: Add publisher location as a third node type to the bipartite graph, testing whether the geography of publishing mirrors or diverges from the geography of authorship.
